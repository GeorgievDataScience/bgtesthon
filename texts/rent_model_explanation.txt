FINAL STRUCTURED VERSION (WITH VISUAL FLOW)

1. Data Source and Structure
The model uses U.S. Consumer Price Index (CPI) data, specifically the category Rent of Primary Residence, published by the Bureau of Labor Statistics.

The dataset consists of monthly observations spanning several decades. These values represent how rental prices change over time relative to a fixed base period.


2. Data Preparation
Monthly values are grouped by year and averaged to produce a representative annual level.

Year-over-year percentage change is then calculated by comparing each year to the previous one.

→ This results in a time series of annual percentage changes, which serves as the foundation of the model.

4. Statistical Measures
From this ordered distribution, key statistics are extracted:

- min / max → full observed range  
- p10 / p25 → lower-end outcomes  
- p50 (median) → central, typical value  
- mean → overall average  
- p75 / p90 → higher-end outcomes  


5. Scenario Construction
The statistics are grouped into four scenarios:

- Optimistic → p10, p25  
- Typical → median (p50), mean  
- Pessimistic → p75, p90  
- Extremes → min, max  


6. Model Application

Initial Rent
     ↓
Apply annual change (Year 1)
     ↓
New Rent
     ↓
Apply same rate again (Year 2)
     ↓
Continue over time

Each year builds on the previous one, producing a projected rent path.


7. Model Outputs

- Rent Growth → monthly rent over time  
- Percentage Increase → relative change  
- Total Spending → cumulative cost  
- Trend Chart → compounding visualization  


8. Interpretation
The model does not predict future outcomes.

It illustrates how rent may evolve if historical patterns persist.
